Chapter 6 — Exponents & Exponential Functions

Concepts & Rules

Exponent rules (must be automatic):

Product of powers: xa · xb = xa+b
Quotient of powers: xa / xb = xa−b
Power of a power: (xa)b = xab
Power of a product: (xy)a = xa · ya
Zero exponent: x0 = 1 (for x ≠ 0)
Negative exponent: x−a = 1 / xa

Scientific notation:

A number written as a × 10n, where 1 ≤ |a| < 10. Move the decimal: each move left = +1 to exponent; each move right = −1.

Example: 0.00045 = 4.5 × 10−4;   23,500,000 = 2.35 × 107

Exponential growth and decay:

Growth: y = a (1 + r)t
Decay: y = a (1 − r)t

a = starting value; r = growth/decay rate (decimal); t = time elapsed.

Common Mistakes

  • Trying to combine bases that aren't the same (e.g., x2 · y3 stays as is).
  • Distributing wrongly: (x + y)2 ≠ x2 + y2. (FOIL it: x2 + 2xy + y2.)
  • Negative exponent ↔ negative answer. x−2 = 1/x2, NOT −x2.
  • Forgetting that anything to the zero power is 1, not 0.
  • Sign of the exponent in scientific notation: small numbers (< 1) have NEGATIVE exponents.

Chapter 7 — Polynomials & Factoring

Concepts & Rules

Operations on polynomials:

  • Add/subtract: combine LIKE TERMS only (same variable AND same exponent).
  • Multiply binomials: FOIL — First, Outer, Inner, Last.

Example: (2x − 3)(x + 5) = 2x2 + 10x − 3x − 15 = 2x2 + 7x − 15

Factoring (the toolkit, in priority order):

  1. Always pull out the GCF first.
  2. Difference of squares: a2 − b2 = (a + b)(a − b)
  3. Perfect-square trinomial: a2 ± 2ab + b2 = (a ± b)2
  4. x2 + bx + c: Find two numbers with product c, sum b.
  5. ax2 + bx + c (a > 1): AC method — find two numbers with product a·c, sum b. Split the middle term, then group.

The AC method, step-by-step (Neil's most common stuck point):

Example: factor 6x2 + 11x − 10. AC = 6 · (−10) = −60. Need two numbers: product −60, sum 11 → +15 and −4. Split: 6x2 + 15x − 4x − 10. Group: 3x(2x + 5) − 2(2x + 5) = (3x − 2)(2x + 5).

Common Mistakes

  • Skipping the GCF check first. Always check.
  • Sign errors when factoring x2 + bx + c. Two numbers with product c, sum b — both signs depend on c and b.
  • Confusing perfect square trinomial with general factoring (the middle term must be exactly 2ab).
  • Stopping too early. After GCF, the remaining factor may still factor further (e.g., 3x3 − 12x → 3x(x2 − 4) → 3x(x+2)(x−2)).
  • In FOIL, forgetting the middle terms or the sign on the last term.

Chapter 8 — Quadratic Functions & Equations

This is the chapter Neil bombed on April 23. Most important chapter for Q4. The teacher's actual test had 7 distinct topic blocks — covered below.

Concepts & Rules

The three forms of a quadratic:

Standard form: y = ax2 + bx + c
Vertex form: y = a(x − h)2 + k    vertex (h, k)
Factored form: y = a(x − r1)(x − r2)    roots r1, r2

Graphing quadratics (the most common Q1 of any quadratics test):

  1. Find axis of symmetry: x = −b / (2a)
  2. Plug it into the equation to find the vertex's y-coordinate.
  3. Build a small table around the axis (2 x-values left, 2 right).
  4. Plot. Direction: a > 0 opens up, a < 0 opens down. Range: y ≥ vertex-y if up, y ≤ vertex-y if down. Domain is always all real numbers.

Vertex form & transformations from y = x2:

y = a(x − h)2 + k
  • h: shifts horizontally — RIGHT if h > 0, LEFT if h < 0 (sign flips!)
  • k: shifts vertically — UP if k > 0, DOWN if k < 0
  • a: vertical stretch (|a| > 1) or compression (|a| < 1); negative a flips over x-axis

Discriminant — counting solutions without solving:

D = b2 − 4ac
  • D > 0: two real solutions (parabola crosses x-axis twice)
  • D = 0: one real solution (parabola touches x-axis at vertex)
  • D < 0: no real solutions (parabola never touches x-axis)

Solving quadratics — pick the right method:

  1. Factoring — fastest if it factors. Set = 0, factor, zero-product property.
  2. Square roots — when there's no x-term. Isolate x2, then ± √.
  3. Completing the square — when a = 1 and b is even. Move c, add (b/2)2 to both sides, factor as (x + b/2)2.
  4. Quadratic formula — always works:
    x = ( −b ± √(b2 − 4ac) ) / (2a)

Word problems — projectile motion (Earth, feet, seconds):

h(t) = −16t2 + v0 t + h0
  • Max height: at t = −b/(2a) (axis of symmetry); plug in to find h.
  • Hits ground: solve h(t) = 0 (take positive t).

Linear vs. quadratic models from a data table:

  • If first differences in y are CONSTANT → linear (y = mx + b).
  • If first differences change but second differences are constant → quadratic. The constant second difference equals 2a.

Common Mistakes

  • Sign of h in vertex form. y = (x + 4)2 = (x − (−4))2, so h = −4 → shift LEFT 4 (not right).
  • Forgetting the ± in the square-roots method. Always two solutions unless one of them is impossible.
  • Quadratic formula: dropping the negative on −b, or losing parentheses around (b2 − 4ac).
  • Discriminant: forgetting that c can be negative, which flips the sign of −4ac.
  • Word problems: not rejecting impossible negative answers (e.g., width = −5).
  • Confusing axis of symmetry x = h with the vertex (which is (h, k)).

Chapter 9 — Radicals, Pythagorean Theorem & Distance

Concepts & Rules

Simplifying square roots:

Pull out perfect-square factors: √48 = √(16·3) = 4√3

Memorize perfect squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144.

Operations:

Multiplication: √a · √b = √(ab)
Division: √a / √b = √(a/b)
Addition (LIKE radicals only): 3√2 + 5√2 = 8√2

3√2 + 5√3 cannot be simplified (different radicands).

Rationalizing the denominator:

Multiply numerator and denominator by the radical that makes the denominator rational.

Example: 4 / √3 = (4 · √3) / (√3 · √3) = 4√3 / 3

Pythagorean theorem (right triangles only):

a2 + b2 = c2   (c = hypotenuse)

Distance and midpoint:

d = √((x2−x1)2 + (y2−y1)2)
M = ( (x1+x2)/2 , (y1+y2)/2 )

Common Mistakes

  • Not simplifying the radical fully. √72 → 6√2, not √72.
  • Adding radicals with different radicands. √2 + √3 ≠ √5.
  • Pythagorean: solving for a leg vs. the hypotenuse. If finding a leg, rearrange to a2 = c2 − b2.
  • Distance formula: forgetting to square the differences before summing.
  • Sign errors with negative coordinates inside the parentheses.
