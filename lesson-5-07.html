5.07 — Interpretation, Correlation & Causation

Six pretest questions came from here. Three skills: interpreting slope/intercept in real-world contexts, reading the correlation coefficient (r), and distinguishing correlation from causation.

10 practice problems ~45 min Pretest Q20-25

Contents

A. Interpreting the y-intercept in context

In a real-world linear equation y = mx + b, the y-intercept b is the value of y when x = 0. In context, it represents the starting value or baseline.

Worked example

Mortgage model: y = 120,000 − 2000x, where x = months and y = money still owed. What does the y-intercept represent?

  • At x = 0 (month zero, before payments), y = 120,000.
  • This is the original amount of the mortgage.
Question pattern: "What does the y-intercept represent?" The answer is always about the starting/initial/baseline value of y — what happens when x = 0.

B. Interpreting the slope as a rate

The slope m tells you how much y CHANGES for each 1-unit increase in x. It's a rate.

  • Positive m: y INCREASES as x increases.
  • Negative m: y DECREASES as x increases.
  • The magnitude of m = how fast it changes.
Worked example

Spaceship velocity: v(t) = 5924 + 312t, where v is velocity (km/min) and t is time (minutes). What is the slope, and what does it represent?

  • Slope m = 312.
  • Positive → velocity INCREASES as time increases.
  • 312 = the rate of increase.
  • "Velocity increases at a rate of 312 km/min for each additional minute."
⚠️ Common trap: students confuse slope with y-intercept. The slope is the COEFFICIENT of x (the number multiplied by x). The y-intercept is the constant TERM (no x attached).

C. The Correlation Coefficient (r)

The correlation coefficient r is a single number between −1 and +1 that summarizes how strongly two variables are linearly related.

r valueMeaning
r = 1Perfect positive linear relationship (dots fall exactly on an upward line)
r = 0.9 to 0.999Very strong positive
r = 0.5 to 0.89Moderate positive
r = 0.0 to 0.49Weak positive (or no relationship)
r = 0No linear relationship
r = −0.5 to −0.89Moderate negative
r = −1Perfect negative linear relationship

Two things tell you about r:

  • Sign (positive or negative): direction of the trend.
  • Magnitude (how close to ±1): strength of the trend.
Worked example

The correlation coefficient between hours studied and test score is r = 0.9976. Describe the relationship.

  • r is positive → as hours studied go up, scores go up.
  • r is very close to +1 → very STRONG relationship.
  • "There is a strong POSITIVE relationship between x and y."

D. Estimating r from a scatter plot

The pretest may show data and ask you to estimate r. Without actually computing it, you can narrow down based on the visual:

  • Dots tightly clustered around a line, going up → r ≈ +0.9
  • Dots vaguely going up but spread out → r ≈ +0.3 to +0.5
  • Dots all over the place, no trend → r ≈ 0
  • Dots loosely going down → r ≈ −0.3 to −0.5
  • Dots tightly along a downward line → r ≈ −0.9

E. Correlation vs. Causation

This is the most-tested concept of 5.07.

  • Correlation: two variables move together — they're statistically related.
  • Causation: one variable DIRECTLY causes the other to change.
⚠️ Correlation does NOT imply causation. Just because two things go up together doesn't mean one caused the other. There could be a third hidden factor, or it could be coincidence.
How to tell which it is:
  • Causation: there's a clear mechanism, a controlled comparison (one thing changes while everything else is held constant), or the relationship has been tested experimentally.
  • Correlation only: data was just observed (not controlled), and the result might be due to a hidden third variable.
Worked example — Causation

"One patient with a cold takes a medicine and the other doesn't. The patient who took medicine recovered faster."

  • There's a DIRECT comparison: treatment vs. no treatment.
  • The difference in outcome can be linked to the treatment.
  • This is causation (the medicine caused the faster recovery).
Worked example — Correlation only

"Marcus puts his water in the refrigerator so it will stay cold."

  • There IS a clear mechanism: refrigerators cool things down.
  • This is actually causation — the refrigerator directly causes the water to stay cold.

Wait, but compare:

"Ice cream sales rise in summer; drowning deaths also rise in summer."

  • Both go up together — they ARE correlated.
  • But ice cream doesn't cause drowning. Heat (summer) causes BOTH.
  • This is correlation only — with a confounding variable (heat).

F. The confounding variable trap

A confounding variable is a hidden third variable that causes BOTH of the variables you're observing. When you see two things correlated, always ask: "could there be a third factor causing both?"

CorrelationConfounding variable (true cause)
Ice cream sales ↔ drowning deathsSummer / hot weather
Shoe size ↔ reading ability (in kids)Age
Number of churches in a city ↔ crime rateCity size / population
Chocolate consumption ↔ Nobel prizes per capitaNational wealth

G. Practice Problems

Problem 1

A mortgage model is y = 120,000 − 2000x (x = months, y = $ remaining). What does the y-intercept represent?

  • A. Amount paid each month
  • B. Monthly interest rate
  • C. Original amount of the mortgage
  • D. Number of months to pay off the mortgage
Show answer
C. At x = 0 (before any payments), y = 120,000. That's the starting balance — the original mortgage amount.
Problem 2

A spaceship's velocity is v(t) = 5924 + 312t (km/min). What is the slope, and what does it mean?

  • A. 5,924 — velocity increases at 5,924 km/min per minute
  • B. −5,924 — velocity decreases at 5,924 km/min per minute
  • C. 312 — velocity increases at 312 km/min per minute
  • D. −312 — velocity decreases at 312 km/min per minute
Show answer
C. The slope is the coefficient of t — that's 312. Positive → velocity increases. 312 km/min per minute of elapsed time.
Problem 3

A correlation coefficient is r = 0.9976. What does this mean?

  • A. Strong positive relationship
  • B. Strong negative relationship
  • C. Weak positive relationship
  • D. Weak negative relationship
Show answer
A. Positive (above 0) → positive trend. Very close to +1 → strong.
Problem 4

Two variables have r = 0.12. The relationship is:

  • A. Strong positive
  • B. Weak positive
  • C. Strong negative
  • D. No relationship at all
Show answer
B. r is positive but close to 0 — weak positive. (Not "no relationship" because r ≠ 0.)
Problem 5

Advertising costs vs. sales data: dots roughly trend upward but spread out moderately. Best estimate for r?

  • A. 0.89
  • B. 0.20
  • C. 0.12
  • D. 0.01
Show answer
A. "Roughly trending upward" with moderate spread → strong positive but not perfect. 0.89 is the closest "strong positive but not extreme" choice.
Problem 6

"One patient with a cold takes medicine, the other does not. The patient who took medicine recovered faster." Is this correlation or causation?

  • A. Correlation — one patient is sick, the other is not
  • B. Causation — both patients need medicine
  • C. Correlation — both patients are sick at the same time
  • D. Causation — the patient who took medicine recovered quicker
Show answer
D. A controlled comparison (one with treatment, one without) directly links the treatment to the faster recovery. This is causation.
Problem 7

"Marcus puts his water in the refrigerator so it will stay cold." Correlation or causation?

  • A. Correlation — not enough observable trials
  • B. Causation — a refrigerator keeps things cold
  • C. Neither — water temperature is independent of refrigeration
  • D. Not enough info
Show answer
B. Refrigeration directly causes the temperature drop — there's a clear mechanism. Causation.
Problem 8

"Towns with more parks have lower crime rates." A headline reads "Parks Reduce Crime." What's wrong with the headline?

  • A. Nothing — the correlation is strong
  • B. It assumes causation from correlation
  • C. Parks should increase crime
  • D. The data is too small
Show answer
B. Correlation alone never proves causation. Possible confounders: wealthier towns have more parks AND less crime; well-funded municipalities invest in both. The correlation is real, but the causal claim isn't supported.
Problem 9

A line of best fit is y = −0.13x + 4.65 over 8 years. What does the slope of −0.13 tell you?

  • A. y decreases by 0.13 per year (units)
  • B. y increases by 0.13 per year
  • C. y is 0.13 at the start
  • D. The line is shifted down by 0.13
Show answer
A. Slope is negative → y is decreasing. Magnitude 0.13 → decreases by 0.13 for each 1-unit increase in x (each year).
Problem 10

Hours of cardio exercise correlate negatively with resting heart rate. Which best describes this relationship?

  • A. Correlation only — they just happen to move together
  • B. Causation — exercise has a known physiological effect on the heart
  • C. Confounded by age
  • D. Not enough info
Show answer
B. Cardio strengthens the heart, lowering resting heart rate — direct biological mechanism. Plus this can be tested experimentally. Causation.

H. What to do next

🎯 Key things to remember

  1. Y-intercept in context = starting value, baseline, the value when x = 0.
  2. Slope in context = rate of change. Positive m → y goes UP. Negative m → y goes DOWN. Magnitude = how fast.
  3. r near +1 = strong positive. r near −1 = strong negative. r near 0 = weak/no relationship.
  4. Correlation ≠ causation. Always ask: is there a confounding variable that explains BOTH?
  5. Causation needs: a clear mechanism OR a controlled comparison.
  6. "Just observed together" = correlation only.
