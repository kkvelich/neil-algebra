Q1 — Comparing two box plots

Two box plots compare the number of male vs female dogs at a daycare each day for a month. Their five-number summaries:

MinQ1MedianQ3MaxOutlier
Males01202550—
Females0561018point at 43
Part A2 pts

Estimate the IQR for the males' data.

Method + answer. IQR = Q3 − Q1 (the width of the box). For the males: 25 − 1 = 24. (IQR is NOT max − min — that's the range.)
Part B2 pts

Estimate the difference between the median values of each data set.

Method + answer. Males median − females median = 20 − 6 = 14.
Part C4 pts

Describe the distribution of each data set, and say whether the mean or the median is the better measure of center for each.

Method + answer.
  • Males: the box and whiskers are uneven — a tiny lower whisker (0→1) and a long upper whisker (25→50) — so the data is skewed right. The long tail would drag the mean up, so the median is the better measure of center.
  • Females: there is a clear outlier at 43, far above the rest. An outlier distorts the mean, so the median is the better measure of center here too.
Rule: use the median whenever data is skewed or has outliers; use the mean only when it's roughly symmetric with no outliers.
Part D2 pts

Give a possible reason for the outlier in the data set.

Sample answer. On one day an unusually large number of female dogs attended — for example a special daycare event, a holiday or weekend rush, or a grooming/adoption promotion that drew many dogs that day. (Any reasonable real-world reason for one unusually high day earns the points.)

What percentage of respondents do NOT like both hamburgers and burritos?

Method + answer. "Not both" = dislikes hamburgers AND dislikes burritos = the 54 cell. 54 ÷ 205 = 0.263 ≈ 26.3%.

What is the marginal relative frequency of all customers who like hamburgers?

Method + answer. Marginal = a row/column TOTAL ÷ grand total. Likes-ham total = 110, so 110 ÷ 205 = 0.537 ≈ 53.7%.

Q3 — Scatter plot & correlation coefficient

A scatter plot shows strawberries picked vs. day in February. The points trend clearly upward (positive), moderately spread around a line. Software reported r = 0.01.

Part A5 pts

Is r = 0.01 an accurate value for this data? Why or why not?

Method + answer. No. An r near 0 means essentially NO linear relationship, but the plot shows a clear upward (positive) trend. A positive trend should give an r much closer to +1 — roughly +0.7 to +0.9 for moderately spread points. So r = 0.01 is not accurate; it contradicts the visible pattern and was likely calculated or entered incorrectly.

Reminder of the r scale: −1 = perfect negative, 0 = none, +1 = perfect positive.
Part B5 pts

Write a scenario that would be a CAUSAL relationship for strawberries picked on the farm.

Sample answer. The day in February only correlates with berries picked — the date doesn't cause it. A causal example: the number of workers picking causes the number of strawberries picked — more pickers directly results in more berries picked. (Also valid: warmer temperature / more sunlight causes more berries to ripen and be picked.)

Key idea: causation means one variable directly makes the other change.

Q4 — Line of best fit from a table

A manager records employees vs. yearly products:

Employees (x)0255075100125150175200
Products (y)1016031046061076091010601210
Part A4 pts

Is there a correlation? Justify your answer.

Method + answer. Yes — a perfect positive correlation. Every time x rises by 25, y rises by exactly 150 (160−10, 310−160, … all = 150). A constant rate of change means the points lie exactly on a line, so r = 1.
Part B3 pts

Write a function that best fits the data.

Method + answer. Slope = Δy/Δx = 150/25 = 6. At x = 0, y = 10, so the y-intercept is 10. y = 6x + 10. (Check x = 200: 6·200 + 10 = 1210 ✓.)
Part C3 pts

What do the slope and the y-intercept indicate?

Method + answer.
  • Slope = 6: each additional employee produces 6 more products per year.
  • y-intercept = 10: with 0 employees, 10 products are still produced — a baseline output (e.g., automated production) before any employees are added.

🔑 Before you submit

  • IQR = Q3 − Q1 (box width); range = max − min. Don't mix them up.
  • Use the median when data is skewed or has an outlier.
  • Marginal = a total ÷ grand total. Conditional = a cell ÷ its row (or column) total — read the wording for which denominator.
  • r matches the picture: upward → near +1, downward → near −1, no pattern → near 0.
  • Line of best fit: slope = Δy/Δx, intercept = value at x = 0. Always interpret them in context, with units.
