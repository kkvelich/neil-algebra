5.05 — Two-Way Frequency Tables

Tables that show how two categorical variables relate (like grade × music preference). The big skill: picking the right denominator for "what % of A are B?" questions.

10 questions ~35 min Due Thursday

Contents

A. What's a two-way frequency table?

A table that cross-tabulates TWO categorical variables. Example: 100 students × favorite course × grade level.

MathEnglishScienceRow Total
Freshmen621018
Sophomores812222
Juniors127524
Col Total26211764

Each cell shows how many students fit BOTH categories (e.g., 8 sophomores liked math). Row and column totals show how many fit EACH category alone. The grand total (64 here) is the total survey size.

B. The four types of frequencies

From a two-way table, you can read or compute four different things. The vocabulary matters — the quiz tests these terms directly.

TypeHow to computeExample (from table above)
Joint frequencyA cell value (raw count)"8 sophomores like math"
Joint relative freq.Cell ÷ grand total8 / 64 = 12.5% — "12.5% of all students are math-loving sophomores"
Marginal frequencyA row or column total"26 students like math"
Marginal relative freq.Row or column total ÷ grand total26 / 64 = 40.6% — "40.6% of all students like math"

There's one more — the most important and most-tested:

C. Conditional Relative Frequency — the big one

A conditional frequency answers questions like:

  • "Of the students who like math, what fraction are juniors?"
  • "Of the juniors, what fraction like math?"

Both involve the same cell (juniors who like math = 12). But the DENOMINATOR is different:

  • "Of the students who like math…" → divide by the COLUMN total for Math (26). 12/26 ≈ 46%.
  • "Of the juniors…" → divide by the ROW total for Junior (24). 12/24 = 50%.

D. The "what % of A are B" denominator rule

The most-missed type of question. Here's the rule:

"What % of A are B?"
The denominator is the total of A.
The numerator is the cell where A AND B meet.
Question phrasing"A" isDenominator
"What % of rock fans are 11-12 grade?"rock fansColumn total for Rock
"What % of 11-12 grade like rock?"11-12 gradeRow total for 11-12 grade
"What % of all students are 11-12 rock fans?"all studentsGrand total
How to read the question wording: the phrase right after "what % of" tells you the denominator. Underline it in your head before computing.
Worked example

Band preference table:

RapRockCountryRow total
Grades 9-10403055125
Grades 11-12602535120
Col total1005590245

Q1: What % of students who like rock are in grades 11-12?

  • "Of students who like rock…" → A = rock fans → denominator = 55 (column total).
  • Numerator = cell where Rock AND 11-12 meet = 25.
  • 25/55 ≈ 45%.

Q2: What % of students in grades 11-12 like rock?

  • "Of students in grades 11-12…" → A = grade 11-12 → denominator = 120 (row total).
  • Numerator = cell where Rock AND 11-12 meet = 25.
  • 25/120 ≈ 21%.

Same cell. Different denominators. Different answers. This is THE trap of 5.05.

E. Filling in missing cells

Questions like "complete the table" use the row total = sum of cells, column total = sum of cells, grand total = sum of all rows = sum of all columns.

Worked example

Complete this table — find the missing cell (under-15 + Walk/Bike):

Walk/BikeBusCarRow Total
Under 15?60?165
15+65??195
Col Total15211098360

Walk/Bike column: 152 total. Under-15 + 15+ = 152. So Under-15 + 65 = 152 → Under-15 Walk/Bike = 87.

Now Under-15 row: 87 + 60 + Under-15 Car = 165 → Under-15 Car = 18.

And so on for the rest.

F. Building a conditional table (row vs column)

Two flavors of conditional relative frequency tables:

  • Row-conditional — divide each cell by its ROW total. Each row sums to 1 (100%).
  • Column-conditional — divide each cell by its COLUMN total. Each column sums to 1 (100%).

The question "conditional on X" tells you which to use. "Conditional on GENDER" usually means divide by row (where gender is the row variable).

G. Practice Problems

Problem 1

In a 100-student survey, the grand total cell shows 100. What term describes the value "100"?

  • A. Joint frequency
  • B. Marginal frequency
  • C. Grand total (a kind of marginal frequency)
  • D. Joint relative frequency
Show answer
C. The grand total is the total of all marginals — it's classified as a marginal frequency / total frequency.
Problem 2

A table shows: column total for "Travel out of state" is 57, grand total is 152. What's the marginal relative frequency for traveling out of state?

  • A. 57 / 100 = 57%
  • B. 57 / 152 ≈ 37.5%
  • C. 12 / 57 ≈ 21%
  • D. 45 / 82 ≈ 55%
Show answer
B. Marginal relative freq. = column total ÷ grand total = 57/152 ≈ 37.5%. (57/100 is the trap — there's no 100 here, only 152.)
Problem 3

Band preference: Rap (Grades 9-10: 40, 11-12: 60), Rock (30, 25), Country (55, 35), Total 245. What % of students who like rock are in grades 11-12?

  • A. 10%
  • B. 21%
  • C. 25%
  • D. 45%
Show answer
D. "Of students who like rock" → denom = column total for Rock = 30 + 25 = 55. Numerator = 25. 25/55 ≈ 45%.
Problem 4

Same band table. What % of students in grades 11-12 like rock?

  • A. 10%
  • B. 21%
  • C. 25%
  • D. 45%
Show answer
B. "Of students in grades 11-12" → denom = row total for 11-12 = 60+25+35 = 120. Numerator = 25. 25/120 ≈ 21%. Different denominator from Problem 3 → different answer.
Problem 5

Travel table: Likes Camping (Likes Travel 52, No Travel 38, total 90). Doesn't Like Camping (36, 74, total 110). Column totals: 88, 112. Grand: 200. What % of students that like to travel out of state do NOT like camping?

Show answer
D. Denom = column total for "Likes Travel" = 88. Numerator = "Likes Travel" AND "Doesn't Camp" = 36. 36/88 ≈ 41%.

Problem 7

A relative frequency table shows: Grade 9 row totals to 0.54. The value 0.18 sits in the Grade 9 + Running cell. Which statement is true?

  • A. In Grade 9, 18 students liked running
  • B. In Grade 9, 27% of students liked volleyball
  • C. In Grade 9, 14 students liked running
  • D. In Grade 9, 18% of students liked running
Show answer
D. 0.18 is a relative frequency = 18%. (Not 18 students — these are decimals/percentages, not counts.)
Problem 8

A two-way table for gender × vacation: Male 38% Hawaii, 12% Paris, row 50%. Female 26% Hawaii, 24% Paris, row 50%. Column totals 64%, 36%, grand 100%. Which row-conditional table is correct?

  • A. Male: 0.76 / 0.24 (rows total 1); Female: 0.52 / 0.48 (rows total 1)
  • B. Raw counts (Male 38/12 = 50, etc.)
  • C. Column-conditional (Male: 0.59 / 0.33, etc.)
  • D. Different scaling
Show answer
A. Row-conditional = divide each cell by row total. Male: 38/50 = 0.76; 12/50 = 0.24. Each row sums to 1.
Problem 9

Survey of 100 students: 60 like math (35 boys, 25 girls). 40 don't like math (15 boys, 25 girls). What % of math-likers are boys?

  • A. 35%
  • B. 50%
  • C. 58%
  • D. 70%
Show answer
C. "Of math-likers" → denom = 60 (column or row total for math). Numerator = boys who like math = 35. 35/60 ≈ 58%.
Problem 10

If a conditional relative frequency table is "conditional on row", each row should sum to:

  • A. The grand total
  • B. 1.0 (or 100%)
  • C. The column total
  • D. The marginal frequency
Show answer
B. Row-conditional divides each cell by its row total → each row's cells sum to 1 (or 100%).

H. What to do next

🎯 Key things to remember

  1. "What % of A are B" → denominator is the total of A. Underline A in the question.
  2. "Of rock fans, what % are 11-12" → divide by column total (rock). "Of 11-12, what % like rock" → divide by row total (11-12). Same cell, different denominators.
  3. Joint = cell. Marginal = row/column total. Add "relative" = divide by something.
  4. Joint relative = cell ÷ grand total. Marginal relative = row/col total ÷ grand total.
  5. Row-conditional: each row sums to 1. Column-conditional: each column sums to 1.
